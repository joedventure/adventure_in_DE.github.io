## History of Magdeburg
***The Founding of Magdeburg and the Medieval Metropolis of Trade***

Located on the banks of the Elbe River, Magdeburg, the capital of Saxony-Anhalt, is an ancient city with over 1,200 years of history. Its past carries multiple chapters of German history, including the era of the East Frankish Kingdom, the German eastward expansion, the Reformation, the industrial age, the era of totalitarianism, and the East German period, making it a microcosm of German history.

Archaeological evidence shows that the area where Magdeburg is located had urban settlements as early as the 5th to 7th centuries AD, while Magdeburg itself was first mentioned in documents in 805 AD. In 929, Otto I, who would later become the Holy Roman Emperor, married Editha, daughter of the King of Wessex, and gave the city to her as a gift. In 962, Otto I was crowned Holy Roman Emperor, and in 967, Magdeburg was elevated to a Catholic Archbishopric, becoming one of the religious centers in the eastern part of the Empire and one of the six archdioceses north of the Alps at that time.

In 1035, Magdeburg was granted the right to hold trade fairs and gradually developed into a medieval trade hub, connecting Brandenburg in the east with important northern cities of the Empire. Additionally, Magdeburg became a member of the Hanseatic League at the end of the 13th century, exporting goods to distant regions. Domestically, together with the neighboring major city of Braunschweig, it became a core member of the Saxon League of Cities, monopolizing the grain trade in the middle Elbe. Consequently, Magdeburg was known as the "Bakehouse of Europe" at the time. The prosperity of trade can also be imagined from the Magdeburg silver coins found as far away as the Faroe Islands.

With the development of trade, the operation of the city itself became highly regulated. The Magdeburg Rights (Magdeburger Recht), established by Otto the Great, regulated market operations, trade taxes, and property ownership, serving as the legal basis for local rulers to grant internal autonomy to towns and villages. The Magdeburg Rights can be considered one of the most important laws in Central Europe during the Middle Ages and spread widely throughout Europe, becoming the foundation for the establishment of many later towns.

***The Reformation and the Fall of the City***

The year 1524 can be considered the year Magdeburg turned from Catholicism to Protestantism. That year, Martin Luther visited Magdeburg to promote his ideas of the Reformation at the invitation of his former classmate, the then-mayor of Magdeburg. After this, only the Cathedral remained Catholic. Following the death of the Archbishop of Magdeburg in 1545, the Cathedral was closed for 20 years until it reopened in 1567 as a Protestant church.

However, the development of Protestantism was not smooth at the time. Holy Roman Emperor Charles V, a staunch supporter of Catholicism, strongly opposed the Reformation, putting great pressure on Protestant forces. Although agreements in 1552 and 1555 temporarily eased religious conflicts, friction between the New and Old denominations continued to escalate after the Peace of Augsburg in 1555, eventually leading to the outbreak of the Thirty Years' War (1618–1648).

This war had a devastating impact on German cities in general, but it was particularly severe for Magdeburg. In May 1631, the Imperial General Johann t'Serclaes, Count of Tilly, led his army to capture Magdeburg, completely looting and burning the entire city. Twenty thousand residents perished in this catastrophe, and Magdeburg became almost an empty city. A census in 1632 showed that Magdeburg had only 449 residents at that time, and the city remained in ruins for several decades, only beginning to recover gradually around 1720.

In 1648, with the signing of the Peace of Westphalia (Westfälischer Friede), the Thirty Years' War largely came to an end, and the ruling power over Magdeburg was transferred to the Electorate of Brandenburg. However, it was not until 1680 that the Electorate of Brandenburg truly gained control of Magdeburg, secularizing it into the Duchy of Magdeburg (Herzogtum Magdeburg) and incorporating it into the territory of the Electorate of Brandenburg. This administrative unit existed until 1806, when it was dissolved due to Napoleon's conquest.

***Industrial Stronghold and Transformation after German Reunification***

Following Napoleon's defeat, Magdeburg returned to the hands of the Kingdom of Prussia in 1814. During the wave of industrialization in the 19th century, Magdeburg rose rapidly to become a stronghold for mechanical engineering and metal processing. During World War II, due to its importance as a military industrial base, the city became a production center for weapons and military supplies; the refineries in the outskirts were primary targets for Allied air raids. In January 1945, a large-scale bombing by the British Royal Air Force destroyed most of the buildings in the city center.

After the end of WWII in 1945, American forces withdrew from Magdeburg, handing it over to the Soviet Union, and it was subsequently incorporated into the planned economy of the East German government. Magdeburg was reshaped into a heavy industry base, focusing on mechanical engineering, the chemical industry, and heavy equipment production. This centralized industrial development model led to the rapid reconstruction of the city but also caused serious environmental pollution problems, such as air and water pollution caused by coal combustion and heavy industrial emissions.

In terms of politics, the East German regime implemented all-encompassing monitoring of citizens' lives. Under the close surveillance of the Ministry for State Security (Stasi), political activities and individual behaviors in the city were largely transparent. The level of control reached an extreme during the Cold War. Among these dark histories, there is one related to Hitler, which I will share when there is a chance in the future.

In 1990, with German reunification, Magdeburg reintegrated into the market economy system. This transition had a huge impact on the city's industrial structure; many state-owned enterprises from the East German era collapsed because they could not adapt to market competition, leading to large-scale unemployment and economic stagnation. Magdeburg's heavy industrial base was weakened as a result, forcing the city to seek diversified economic development, such as promoting service and innovative industries, while continuing to play an important role in the German economy.

## Magdeburg Cathedral (Magdeburger Dom)
![Dom]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Magdeburg/MD_Dom.jpg)
The history of Magdeburg Cathedral dates back to the 10th century. After ascending the throne in 936, Otto the Great founded the Monastery of St. Maurice in Magdeburg in 937. At that time, Magdeburg was located on the eastern frontier of the Holy Roman Empire. However, Otto the Great decided to make Magdeburg his primary center of rule, hoping to spread Christianity to the surrounding Slavic peoples and bring them under the empire's control.

In 955, after defeating the Hungarians at the Battle of Lechfeld, Otto the Great established numerous dioceses on the empire's eastern border. He ordered the reconstruction of St. Maurice's Monastery into a Romanesque church to serve as a royal mausoleum and religious center. In 962, he was crowned Holy Roman Emperor, and in 968, he further elevated Magdeburg's status by establishing the Archbishopric of Magdeburg.

During the 11th and 12th centuries, the cathedral suffered damage due to structural issues and several fires. In the early 13th century, Archbishop Albrecht began presiding over its reconstruction, which eventually resulted in a magnificent Gothic cathedral. This project took 313 years to complete, finishing in 1520. During the Reformation in the 16th century, influenced by the preaching of Martin Luther, Magdeburg turned to Protestantism, and the cathedral officially became a Protestant church in 1567.

However, during the Thirty Years' War, the city of Magdeburg was almost completely destroyed. Nevertheless, the cathedral was not entirely ruined. Approximately 4,000 citizens sought refuge here, and the pastor at the time, Reinhard Bake, knelt before the Imperial commander Tilly to plead for mercy, successfully saving both the refugees and the cathedral. During the Napoleonic occupation, the cathedral was used as a warehouse and for military purposes. It was not until Prussia regained control that Magdeburg Cathedral was slowly restored with the support of the Prussian King.

With the reconstruction and modernization of Magdeburg, the cathedral has become an iconic landmark, continuing to witness the city's historical changes over a millennium.

## Magdeburg Hemispheres (Magdeburger Halbkugeln)
![Dom]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Magdeburg/MD_Halbkugeln.jpg)
Many people have heard of the Magdeburg Hemispheres experiment. This experiment was first demonstrated by Otto von Guericke in 1654 at the Imperial Diet in Regensburg. He joined two metal hemispheres tightly together and pumped out the air inside to create a near-vacuum. Because the internal pressure nearly vanished while the external atmospheric pressure continued to act on the surface, the hemispheres could not be easily pulled apart even by horses. This experiment visually demonstrated the existence and power of atmospheric pressure, marking an important milestone in proving atmospheric physics.

Since von Guericke was a native of Magdeburg, he also publicly recreated this experiment in the city during his tenure as mayor. Consequently, as you stroll through the streets of Magdeburg, you can often spot pairs of hemispheres in various parts of the city. A monument to the Magdeburg Hemispheres experiment is also located at Ratswaageplatz in the city center to commemorate this achievement.

As for von Guericke himself, he was born into a prominent family and received a private education until the age of 15, when he entered the University of Leipzig to study law and philosophy. He later continued his studies at the universities of Helmstedt, Jena, and Leiden. His experience at the University of Leiden deepened his understanding and interest in mathematics and physics.

In 1646, von Guericke began serving as the Mayor of Magdeburg, a position he held for over 30 years until his retirement in 1678. Three years before he began his mayoral career, the Italian physicist Evangelista Torricelli created a vacuum above a column of mercury, overturning the traditional view that a vacuum could not exist and proving the existence of atmospheric pressure. This discovery deeply impacted von Guericke, prompting him to dedicate himself to related research and leading to his invention of the world's first vacuum pump in 1650. It was this pump that made the Magdeburg Hemispheres experiment possible.

Those interested in learning more can also visit the Otto von Guericke Museum.

## Famous Sculptures in the Market Square
![Statue]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Magdeburg/MD_Statue.jpg)
There are three famous sculptures in Magdeburg's Market Square: the Roland Statue, the Magdeburg Horseman, and the Column of the Golden-Chained Stag.

1. Roland was a military leader under Charlemagne. His heroic deeds at the Battle of Roncevaux Pass were widely circulated in the Middle Ages and passed down through the French epic "The Song of Roland." Due to his image as a loyal warrior and defender of justice, Roland is seen as a guardian of civic freedom and judicial justice. In addition to the Roland Statue in Magdeburg (recorded to have existed since 1419), he can be found in many other European cities.

2. The original Magdeburg Horseman (Magdeburger Reiter) was completed in 1240, featuring Otto the Great as a knight accompanied by two ladies. The one currently in the Market Square is a replica, while the original statue is preserved in the Cultural History Museum of Magdeburg (Kulturhistorisches Museum Magdeburg). The creator and the exact reason for the statue's commission remain unclear. However, it is certain that this statue represents imperial power and serves as a symbol of city autonomy and a landmark of Magdeburg.

3. The Column of the Golden-Chained Stag (Hirschsäule) likely first appeared around 1425. Theories about its symbolism vary. One legend says that Charlemagne once caught a stag, placed a golden necklace on it, and released it. Engraved on the necklace was the sentence: "Dear hunter, let me live, and I will give you my necklace" (Lieber Jäger, laß mich leben, ich will dir mein Halsband geben). Another theory suggests that the stag is a symbol of the promise of heaven and the Christian faith, echoing the other two statues that represent secular power.

## The Green Citadel (Grüne Zitadelle)
![Grüne Zitadele]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Magdeburg/MD_Grüne Zitadele.jpg)
Friedensreich Hundertwasser, also known as Mr. Hundertwasser, was a famous Austrian artist. His work is known for its abstract style, vibrant colors, and organic curves.

His most famous work is the Hundertwasserhaus in Vienna, Austria. His designs integrate the concepts of nature, sustainability, and harmony between humans and the environment. The building features green roofs and plantings, symbolizing the symbiosis of nature and architecture. The facade design uses curves and asymmetrical windows, rejecting the mechanical aesthetics of straight lines to showcase a lively and vivid vitality.

In addition to Vienna, buildings designed by Hundertwasser can be seen in several places in Germany, one of which is the Green Citadel located in the center of Magdeburg. If you have the chance to visit, it is well worth a stop.
