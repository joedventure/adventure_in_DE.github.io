## History of Jüterbog
Could a city's name really be decided by a random passerby and a goat? 🐑

Jüterbog is located in the state of Brandenburg, about 70 kilometers south of Berlin. There is a charming story behind its name: legend has it that when the city was completed, no one knew what to call it. The troubled citizens came up with an idea to wait by the city gate and name the town after the first person to enter that day. As it happened, an elderly woman named Jutta arrived leading a buck (Bock), and thus "Jüterbog" was born. The black buck subsequently became the city’s coat of arms.

Digressions aside, Jüterbog’s history dates back to 1007. Under the jurisdiction of the Archbishop of Magdeburg during the Middle Ages, it developed into a prosperous regional trading center, leaving behind many precious architectural heritages such as the Town Hall from the late 13th century, the city walls completed in 1300, and St. Nicholas Church. These buildings remain the most important landmarks of the city today.

Furthermore, this location played a pivotal role in the Reformation. In 1517, Johann Tetzel sold indulgences here on a grand scale. This controversial practice directly inspired Martin Luther to publish his famous Ninety-five Theses in nearby Wittenberg, sparking the beginning of the Protestant Reformation.

In the following centuries, due to its strategic location, Jüterbog gradually became a military stronghold. During the Thirty Years' War, the famous commander Wallenstein stationed his troops here; in 1756, King Frederick II of Prussia used it as a starting point to launch the Seven Years' War. After being incorporated into Prussia in 1815, the city's military character intensified. In 1864, Germany's first artillery firing range opened here, making it the largest garrison city in the country during the German Empire. This militarized destiny continued through Nazi Germany and the Cold War until the last Russian soldiers withdrew in 1994.

Today, Jüterbog features a well-preserved old town and many medieval red-brick buildings, making it an ancient city near Berlin well worth a visit.

## Medieval City Walls and Gates
![Jüterbog Dammtor]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Jüterbog/Jüterbog_Dammtor.jpg)
A highlight of Jüterbog is its medieval city walls built of red brick and its well-preserved gates, such as Zinnaer Tor to the north, Dammtor to the west, and Neumarkttor to the east. The classic view of Jüterbog is located at Dammtor. Look closely, and you will find a plaque on the gate that reads: "He who gives his children bread and suffers want himself afterward, should be struck dead with a club" (Wer seinen Kindern gibt das Brot und leidet nachmals selber Noth, den schlage man mit der Keule todt).

This sentence is not meant to advocate violence but serves as a symbol. According to legend, a once-wealthy cloth merchant gave all his property to his sons while he was alive, only to become destitute due to their neglect. Before his death, he left only this plaque engraved with the motto. Perhaps this reflects the medieval philosophy of emphasizing prudent financial management!

## Jüterbog Town Hall (Rathaus Jüterbog)
![Jüterbog Rathaus]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Jüterbog/Jüterbog Rathaus.jpg)
The history of Jüterbog Town Hall began in 1285 when Archbishop Ernst von Magdeburg granted a piece of land on the square to the city, laying the foundation for this building. Completed in 1507, the structure blends various architectural styles, including typical Gothic arcades and a Saxon-style long banquet hall.

On the northeast side of the Town Hall stands a statue of St. Maurice (St. Mauritius), the city's patron saint, symbolizing that Jüterbog was still under the influence of the Archbishop of Magdeburg at the time, who commissioned the sale of indulgences in the spring of 1517. On the second floor, the Princely Chamber (Fürstenzimmer) is where the military leader Wallenstein and King Frederick the Great of Prussia decided the fate of the city. This illustrates the historical significance of the Jüterbog Town Hall.

Today, this historic building still serves as the administrative center for the city government, while the historically significant Princely Chamber has become the Mayor's office.

## St. Nicholas Church (Nikolaikirche)
![Jüterbog_Nikolaikirche]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Jüterbog/Jüterbog_Nikolaikirche.jpg)
St. Nicholas Church in Jüterbog is one of the best-preserved Gothic hall churches in the Brandenburg region, first explicitly mentioned in documents in 1307. The church is characterized by two towers of different shapes, showcasing the wealth of the town at that time.

St. Nicholas Church served as an important stage during the Reformation. In 1519, Franz Günther, a student of Martin Luther, preached reformist ideas here, which triggered strong opposition from Franciscan monks and eventually led to a gag order. However, the famous theologian Thomas Müntzer subsequently took over the preaching and engaged in fierce debates with the head of the Franciscans. Historically significant is that in a letter of complaint written by the Franciscans to the Bishop of Brandenburg, these followers of the new doctrine were referred to as "Lutherans" for the first time in history. It wasn't until 1540, when the Archbishop allowed the town free religious practice, that St. Nicholas Church officially became a Protestant (Evangelical) church.

## The Story and Legend of Johann Tetzel
![Tezel-Kasten]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Jüterbog/Jüterbog_Kiste.jpg)
During the Reformation, St. Nicholas Church in Jüterbog was a hotspot for the sale of indulgences. The famous Dominican friar Johann Tetzel used a highly provocative jingle to attract believers to buy indulgences: "As soon as the coin in the coffer rings, the soul from purgatory springs" (Wenn das Geld im Kasten klingt, die Seele in den Himmel springt). He claimed that by donating money to fund the reconstruction of St. Peter's Basilica in Rome, one could shorten the time spent suffering in purgatory. However, Tetzel's massive commercial success in Jüterbog completely infuriated Martin Luther in Wittenberg, prompting him to indignantly draft the famous Ninety-five Theses in 1517, igniting the flames of the Reformation.

Regarding Tetzel, the most widely circulated folk legend tells of a knight who purchased an indulgence from him in advance and subsequently robbed Tetzel, claiming he was exempt from guilt. It is said that after Tetzel was robbed, his money chest (Tetzel-Kasten), once full of gold coins, became empty. This important historical artifact representing the beginning of indulgences and the Reformation can still be seen in St. Nicholas Church in Jüterbog.

## Zinna Abbey (Kloster Zinna)
Not far north of Jüterbog lies the famous Zinna Abbey. It was originally a monastery for men established in 1170 by the Cistercians with the help of the Archbishop of Magdeburg. Following the wave of the Reformation, Zinna Abbey was dissolved in 1553 and briefly became a country villa for the Brandenburg rulers.

After the Seven Years' War, King Frederick the Great of Prussia ordered the establishment of a small town here in 1764 to revitalize the national economy. He provided free housing, farmland, relocation subsidies, and a ten-year tax exemption to attract textile workers from Saxony and Upper Lusatia to produce cloth and silk for the army. Although the weavers later failed to become wealthy due to the unstoppable wave of industrialization, they remained grateful to the King and erected the "Old Fritz" monument in the center of the square on the town's 100th anniversary. The town was officially named Kloster Zinna in 1902 and later incorporated into Jüterbog.

Today, Zinna Abbey houses a museum that showcases the history of the monastery and the subsequent development of the town. It also preserves ancient hand looms and related equipment, allowing us to experience the working conditions of the weavers at that time. Additionally, do not miss the show factory of the famous herbal liqueur, Zinnaer Klosterbruder. With a museum ticket, you can redeem a free tasting sample. While learning about the production process, you can also personally taste this famous local spirit!

## Higher Air Force Technical School (Höhere Fliegertechnische Schule)
![Jüterbog HTFS]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Jüterbog/Jüterbog_HTFS.jpg)
During the Wilhelmine era, Jüterbog was renowned as Germany's largest garrison city. However, during the interwar period, Germany was prohibited from having an air force under the Treaty of Versailles. Although secret military cooperation between Germany and the Soviet Union had already begun during the Weimar period, the Nazis expanded the military more aggressively after taking power. Against this backdrop, Reich Minister of Aviation Hermann Göring established the Technical School of the German Commercial Pilot School (Technische Schule der Deutschen Verkehrsfliegerschule) in Jüterbog in 1933 under extreme secrecy. This facility, initially disguised as a civilian institution, primarily aimed to train technicians and engineers for maintaining military aircraft. Under the leadership of the first commander, Kurt Studen, 1,500 students were trained here in the first year.

In 1940, as the original technical school moved to Warsaw, the Higher Air Force Technical School (HFTS) from Berlin moved in. The HFTS undertook more specialized military tasks, including training technicians for newly developed equipment, retraining officers and soldiers unfit for battle as mechanical engineers, and conducting cutting-edge aeronautical research. It even trained officers from countries such as Finland, Croatia, Bulgaria, and Romania.

On April 20, 1945, the Soviet Red Army occupied the site. Since the German army did not have time to blow up the buildings before retreating, the facilities were preserved intact. During the subsequent Cold War, the complex was converted into an armored and artillery training center for the Soviet Army, making Jüterbog one of the largest Soviet garrison sites outside of Russia.

Following the fall of the Iron Curtain and the withdrawal of Soviet troops, this complex has remained vacant. Although the area is currently closed to the public, exquisite murals depicting agricultural and aerospace themes from the Soviet era remain inside the buildings. Next to the main complex, there is a small gymnasium and a football field that are still used by local sports clubs. While the gymnasium itself is protected and will not be demolished, there are currently no plans for renovation.
