## History of Wünsdorf 
Wünsdorf is located about 40 kilometers south of Berlin in the present-day state of Brandenburg. For a long time, it was known as the "Forbidden City" (die verbotene Stadt) or even "Little Moscow," names tied to its military use during the Soviet era. As a starting point for exploring Soviet relics in and around Berlin, Wünsdorf is undoubtedly the best choice—not to mention that the guided tours here are among the most interesting outdoor activities I've recently experienced.

The name is believed to be related to the Slavic word for water (wun/wyn). Originally, there were two villages, Nächst-Wünsdorf and Fern-Wünsdorf, situated along the banks connecting two nearby lakes. In 1545, the two villages were formally incorporated into Zossen and merged in 1874, becoming the largest municipality in the District of Teltow (Kreis Teltow) at the time.

At the end of the 19th century, the Prussian army used the area as a training ground and established an infantry school in 1906, which attracted many civil servants and merchants to settle here. Starting in 1935, the 5th Panzer Division was stationed here, and the Supreme Command of the German Army (OKH) moved into the Maybach I/II bunker complexes and the Zeppelin communications bunker from August 1939 until 1945.

After the end of World War II, these facilities were taken over by the Soviet Union. In 1954, the headquarters of the Group of Soviet Forces in Germany (GSSD / ГСВГ) was established here, making it the largest Red Army garrison outside the Soviet Union. The area was heavily guarded, functioning like a secluded "state within a state," which earned it the nickname "Forbidden City." However, to serve the personnel and their families, a dedicated train ran daily between Wünsdorf and Moscow until 1994.

Following German reunification, the Soviet troops withdrew peacefully, and the former Forbidden City was converted for civilian use. Today, many residential areas have been built, turning it into a quiet place to live. The former Soviet military headquarters can now be visited via guided tours to explore its mysterious past.

## Former Soviet Military Base in Wünsdorf (Das Haus der Offiziere)
![Wünsdorf Haus der Offiziere]({{ site.baseurl }}/_image/Wünsdorf/Wünsdorf.jpg)
Several locations in Wünsdorf can still be visited through guided tours, including the Maybach I/II bunkers, the Zeppelin communications bunker, and "Das Haus der Offiziere" (The House of Officers), which was formerly used for military training.

The tour I joined this time was for the "Haus der Offiziere," which includes a swimming pool, parade ground, theater, and dormitories. According to the guide, this vast area could be purchased for 10 million euros. While that might sound reasonable at first, it is a protected heritage site, meaning the owner must perform regular maintenance—so I think it's more practical not to entertain that idea! 😅

The meeting point for the tour is the Lenin statue in front of the parade ground's reviewing stand. In truth, if you look closely, you can still find several Lenin statues in Germany, but few are as perfectly preserved as this one. This statue wasn't always here; it was moved elsewhere after WWII and only later reinstated at this spot.

A short walk along the path around the parade ground leads to the first highlight of the tour: the swimming pool. There is both an indoor and an outdoor pool. The indoor pool was primarily for officers; the showers are just like modern ones in Germany—no partitions! 😳 Overall, it is quite well-maintained.

The outdoor pool is a different story; it now looks like a basin overgrown with grass. During the Wehrmacht era, it had a retractable roof, but this was dismantled during the Soviet period. Nearby are outdoor changing rooms; since there aren't many mosquitoes in Germany, they should still be functional.

Continuing further inside, you reach the theater. This building was originally an indoor training hall but was converted into a theater during the Soviet era. Inside, there is a foyer, an assembly hall (I'm not entirely sure of its specific use, but it looks exactly like those in Taiwan, complete with a piano on the stage), and the theater itself, featuring many cinema-style seats and the emblem of the Western Group of Forces (Западная группа войск) above the stage.

Diagonally across from the theater square stands an old house hidden in the forest. Since the era of Kaiser Wilhelm, this was the residence of military commanders. Friedrich Paulus, who led the German 6th Army during the Battle of Stalingrad, also once lived here.

Finally, we walked back to the main building, which housed officers and physical education instructors. It consists mostly of double rooms, providing accommodation for a total of 120 people. Upstairs are many meeting rooms, some featuring Soviet-style murals. There is also a small room called the "Raum der Stille" (Room of Silence), which was formerly a Soviet museum for the Great Patriotic War, featuring a 360-degree mural and percussion elements to re-enact the Battle of Berlin and commemorate fallen soldiers.
